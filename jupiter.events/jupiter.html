The Eternal Auroras of Jupiter

Alignment: Jupiter’s auroras form when charged particles from the solar wind, as well as particles from its volcanic moon Io, are funneled along Jupiter’s powerful magnetic field lines into its polar regions, colliding with atmospheric gases.
Appearance: From space, they appear as brilliant glowing ovals of ultraviolet and infrared light around Jupiter’s poles. Unlike Earth’s auroras, Jupiter’s are far more intense, sometimes hundreds of times brighter, and can include shimmering curtains and arcs of blue, violet, and pink light.
Rarity: Auroras on Jupiter are not rare events—they are nearly constant due to the planet’s immense magnetosphere and Io’s continuous volcanic activity supplying charged particles.
