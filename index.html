Who Founded the solar system ?

For centuries,people believed the Earth was the center of the universe(the geocentric model).


Nicolaus Copernicus, a Polish astronomer, proposed the heliocentric model in 1543, where the Earth and other planets orbit the Sun.


This theory was a major turning point in science, known as the Copernican Revolution.


Later scientists like Johannes Kepler and Galileo Galile provided further evidence supporting the heliocentric model. Kepler discovered that planets orbit in elliptical paths, and Galileo's observations with the telescope proved that not all celestial bodies orbited the Earth.
why sun is so hot ?
Intense pressure:

The sun's immense mass creates incredible gravitational forces that generate immense pressure in its core.

Hydrogen to helium:

This pressure is so strong that it forces hydrogen atoms to combine, or "fuse," into helium atoms.

Energy release:

During this fusion process, a significant amount of energy is released, which is what makes the sun so hot and bright.

Why saturn have rings ?
Roche Limit & Tidal Forces
The Roche limit is an invisible boundary around a planet where its gravitational pull is strong enough to overcome an object's own gravity.
Tidal Forces:
When an object crosses this limit, the gravitational force on the side facing the planet is much stronger than on the far side, creating intense tidal forces that tear the object apart.
Shattering & Debris
An icy moon, a comet, or an asteroid entered Saturn's Roche limit, and was catastrophically broken apart by the planet's overwhelming gravity.
Formation of Rings
The fragments from the shattered object spread out into a disc around Saturn, continuing to collide and break into smaller pieces.
why every planet moves at different speed
Each planet’s speed around the Sun depends on how far it is from the Sun — and how strong the Sun’s gravity pulls on it.
🌞 Closer = Faster
Inner planets (like Mercury and Venus) are pulled more strongly by the Sun’s gravity. To avoid falling in, they must move faster — like Mercury, which orbits at 47 km/s.
🌌 Farther = Slower
Outer planets (like Neptune and Uranus) feel weaker gravity. They move slower, taking longer to complete an orbit — Neptune takes 165 Earth years to go around once.
What’s the coldest place in the solar system?
The coldest known spot isn’t on Pluto or Neptune — it’s Hermite Crater, near the Moon’s north pole. Temperatures there plunge to −248°C (−415°F), colder than anywhere else we've measured. 🧊 Why So Cold?
Permanent Shadow:
Hermite Crater never sees sunlight due to its steep walls and polar location.
No Atmosphere:
The Moon lacks an atmosphere to trap heat, so shadowed areas become cosmic freezers.
Deep Freeze Zone:
These conditions allow ancient ice to survive — making Hermite a key target for future lunar missions.
