Mercury
Events
Mercury, the smallest planet in the solar system, has a diameter of 4,879 km—about 38% of Earth's. It's the closest to the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of 58 million km, completing a year in just 88 Earth days. Mercury rotates slowly, with a day lasting 59 Earth days, but its year is shorter due to tidal locking effects. Its surface is heavily cratered, resembling the Moon, with extreme temperature swings.
MOONS
Mercury has no moons
ATMOSPHERE
Mercury has an extremely thin atmosphere (exosphere), composed mainly of helium (42%), sodium (29%), and traces of hydrogen and oxygen, with surface pressure about 10^-14 times Earth's. This leads to wild temperature extremes: daytime highs up to 427°C (800°F) near the equator, and nighttime lows to -173°C (-280°F). No significant weather occurs, but solar radiation and micrometeorites constantly bombard the surface.
Exploration History
Exploration began with NASA's Mariner 10 in 1974-1975, which flew by three times, mapping 45% of the surface and revealing its cratered terrain. The MESSENGER spacecraft (2004-2015) orbited Mercury for four years, providing detailed data on its composition, magnetic field (a weak one generated by its iron core), and geology. It confirmed water ice in permanently shadowed polar craters.
Venus
Events
Venus is the second planet from the Sun in our solar system, orbiting at an average distance of about 108 million kilometers. It's often called Earth's "sister planet" due to its similar size (diameter of 12,104 km, about 95% of Earth's mass).
MOONS
Venus is a terrestrial planet with no moons
ATMOSPHERE
Venus has a thick atmosphere, mostly carbon dioxide (96%), with clouds of sulfuric acid that trap heat in a runaway greenhouse effect. Atmospheric pressure at the surface is about 92 times that of Earth, equivalent to being 900 meters underwater on our planet. This dense air causes extreme winds, but they move slowly at ground level (about 10 km/h). The planet experiences perpetual cloud cover, reflecting sunlight and making it the brightest object in the night sky after the Moon.
Exploration History
Venus has been explored by missions like NASA's Magellan (1990-1994), which mapped 98% of its surface via radar. Soviet Venera probes landed in the 1970s-1980s, surviving briefly before succumbing to heat and pressure. A notable quirk is its retrograde rotation—spinning backward compared to most planets, possibly from a past collision. Future missions, like ESA's EnVision (planned for 2030s), aim to study its geology and potential past habitability. Venus's extreme conditions make it a cautionary tale for climate change on Earth.
Earth
Events
Earth is the third planet from the Sun, orbiting at an average distance of about 150 million kilometers. It's the only known planet with life, with a diameter of 12,742 km and a mass of about 5.97 × 10^24 kg. MOONS
Earth has one natural satellite, the Moon
ATMOSPHERE
Earth's atmosphere is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases, including argon and carbon dioxide. It supports weather systems driven by the water cycle, with temperatures ranging from -89°C in Antarctica to 58°C in deserts. The greenhouse effect keeps the average surface temperature at 15°C, enabling liquid water. Climate zones include tropics, temperate regions, and polar areas, influenced by ocean currents and wind patterns.
Exploration History
Earth has been explored extensively via satellites, probes, and human missions, with the International Space Station orbiting since 1998. A unique aspect is its biosphere, teeming with millions of species, from microbes to megafauna, in ecosystems like rainforests and coral reefs. The planet's hydrosphere and biosphere interact in the carbon cycle, regulating climate. Earth's habitability is rare in the universe, with ongoing studies on climate change and sustainability.
Mars
Events
Mars, the fourth planet from the Sun, is a terrestrial world about half the size of Earth, with a diameter of 6,792 km. It orbits the Sun at an average distance of 228 million km, taking 687 Earth days for one year.
MOONS
Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos
ATMOSPHERE
Mars has a thin atmosphere, mostly carbon dioxide (95%), with traces of nitrogen and argon, exerting only 0.6% of Earth's atmospheric pressure. This leads to extreme cold, with average surface temperatures around -65°C (-85°F), ranging from -87°C at the poles to 20°C in equatorial summers. Dust storms can engulf the planet, and water exists as ice beneath the surface or in polar caps, but liquid water is unstable due to low pressure.
Exploration History
Human exploration began with flybys in the 1960s, like NASA's Mariner 4 in 1965, revealing a cratered, lifeless world. Landers such as Viking 1 (1976) conducted soil tests for life signs (none found). Rovers like Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance have mapped terrains, analyzed rocks, and searched for ancient microbial life. Orbiters like Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter provide high-resolution imagery.
Jupiter
Events
Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in our solar system, with a diameter of about 143,000 km (11 times Earth's) and a mass 318 times greater. It's a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, orbiting at an average distance of 778 million kilometers from the Sun. Jupiter takes 12 Earth years for one orbit but rotates rapidly, completing a day in just 10 hours. The planet's strong gravity influences asteroids and comets, acting as a cosmic "vacuum cleaner."
MOONS
It has at least 95 known moons, including the four largest Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto.
ATMOSPHERE
Jupiter's atmosphere is thick and turbulent, with zones of rising and sinking gases creating colorful belts and zones. The iconic Great Red Spot is a massive storm (larger than Earth) that's raged for centuries, possibly a high-pressure anticyclone. Winds can reach 600 km/h, and the planet emits more heat than it receives from the Sun due to gravitational contraction. Temperatures drop from -145°C at the top to thousands of degrees deeper in, with auroras visible at the poles.
Exploration History
Jupiter has been studied by probes like NASA's Pioneer 10 (1973), Voyager (1979), Galileo (1995-2003), and Juno (since 2016), which revealed its complex weather and magnetic environment. A standout feature is its faint ring system, made of dust from its moons. Jupiter's role in the solar system includes protecting inner planets from impacts. Future missions, like ESA's JUICE (2023 launch), will explore its moons for potential habitability. Jupiter's sheer scale and dynamism make it a key to understanding gas giant formation.
Saturn
Events
Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second-largest in our solar system, with a diameter of about 120,500 km (9.5 times Earth's) and a mass 95 times greater. It's a gas giant primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, orbiting at an average distance of 1.4 billion kilometers from the Sun. Saturn takes 29 Earth years for one orbit but rotates quickly, with a day lasting about 10.7 hours.
MOONS
It has at least 146 known moons, including Titan (the largest, with a thick atmosphere) and Enceladus (known for geysers)
ATMOSPHERE
Saturn's atmosphere is thick and layered, with winds up to 1,800 km/h creating hexagonal storm patterns at the north pole and giant hurricanes. The planet's low density (less than water) means it would float if placed in a giant bathtub. Temperatures range from -185°C at the top to hotter depths, with seasonal changes due to its 27-degree axial tilt. Saturn emits 2.5 times more heat than it receives from the Sun, likely from helium rain in its interior.
Exploration History
Saturn was visited by NASA's Pioneer 11 (1979), Voyager (1980-1981), Cassini (2004-2017), and Dragonfly (en route to Titan since 2023). Cassini revealed the rings' complex structure and moon geology. A unique aspect is its faint auroras and spokes in the rings, caused by electrostatic forces. Saturn's rings and moons make it a mini solar system, offering insights into planetary formation. Future studies focus on its potential for extraterrestrial life.

Neptune
Events
Neptune is the eighth and outermost planet from the Sun in our solar system, orbiting at an average distance of about 4.5 billion kilometers. It's an ice giant, similar to Uranus, with a diameter of 49,528 km (about 3.9 times Earth's) and a mass 17 times that of Earth. Discovered in 1846 by Urbain Le Verrier and Johann Galle, it's named after the Roman god of the sea. Neptune has no solid surface and rotates quickly, completing a day in about 16 hours, while its year lasts 165 Earth years.
MOONS
It possesses 16 known moons, with Triton—the largest—being geologically active and having a retrograde orbit.
ATMOSPHERE
Neptune's atmosphere is primarily hydrogen (80%) and helium (19%), with methane giving it a deep blue hue by absorbing red light. It features the solar system's strongest winds, reaching 2,100 km/h (1,300 mph), and large storm systems like the Great Dark Spot (a hurricane-like vortex). The planet's average temperature is -200°C (-328°F), but it radiates more heat than it receives due to internal energy. Its atmosphere lacks significant weather patterns visible from afar, but methane clouds create hazes.
Exploration History
The only spacecraft to visit Neptune was NASA's Voyager 2 in 1989, which captured images of its moons, rings, and atmosphere, revealing Triton as the coldest known object in the solar system (-235°C). A unique trait is its retrograde rotation (backward spin), possibly from a massive impact. Future missions are proposed, but none are imminent. Neptune's extreme distance and faintness make it a distant, enigmatic world, with Triton potentially harboring subsurface oceans. Its isolation highlights the diversity of gas giants in the outer solar system.
